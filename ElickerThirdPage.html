Additional Cover Crop Information

Cover Crop Information To Consider

An important factor that should influence what cover crops you plant is the nutrient profile of your soil and what nutrients you might desire for your field. For instance, cotton plants are known for depleting nitrogen from the soil. If you plan to plant another crop of cotton next spring, you should consider ways to restore nitrogen back into your fields. Despite fertilizer seeming like an attractive alternative, fertilizers can be expensive, and have the risk of flowing in runoff into bodies of water like lakes and rivers. This influx of nitrogen could potentially cause algae blooms in a process called eutrophication. If you instead planted cover crops to fixate nitrogen into your soil, such as legumes, the risk of harmful runoff is not present. For a cotton farmer, a great legume to plant is Austrian winter peas for their nitrogen fixing properties and their seed harvest value.

Environmental Influence On Cover Crops

Environmental conditions unique to your field should influence what cover crops you plant. You should plant cover crops to meet the needs of whatever challenges your field faces. For a cotton farmer struggling with high winds that cause erosion, wheat could be planted which is known for its ability to mitigate erosion. To combat weeds that are sprouting up across your field, cereal rye is effective due to it discouraging weed growth.

Summary

Leaving fields idle can cause a plethora of negative effects, however, many of these challenges can be prevented and reversed through the use of cover crops in the off seasons. It is essential that you consider the time of year, what nutrients your soil lacks, and what environmental conditions are present in your field when formulating a blend of cover crops. For a cotton farmer, a mix of legumes, such as Austrian winter peas, will help restore a balance of nitrogen to your field. Grasses such as cereal rye and wheat can be used to mitigate erosion and weeds. Instead of leaving an empty field every winter when the main crop you're growing is out of season, use that land for planting cover crops to enrich and maintain your soil.
